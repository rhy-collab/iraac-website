# Who We Are: IRAAC's Story So Far

Every organisation has a story, and IRAAC's is still very much being written. Rather than wait until every detail is polished before we say anything at all, we wanted to share where we are right now — honestly, and in our own words — because we think our community deserves to see the organisation as it actually is, not just a finished version of it somewhere down the track.

IRAAC is an Aboriginal Community Organisation operating within the New South Wales Local Decision Making framework. In the simplest terms, that means we exist to strengthen community, culture and self-determination, and we do that through a combination of direct programs and an increasing focus on building strong, accountable governance behind the scenes. We're led by a Board, chaired by our Chairperson and supported by our Secretary, and like any organisation genuinely trying to grow into its responsibilities, we're doing this work in the open rather than pretending we've already arrived.

Right now, IRAAC runs four community programs, each aimed at a different part of what our community needs: MCC, which supports other Aboriginal Community Organisations to build their own governance and administration capability; YouthScape, focused on connecting young people with culture, opportunity and support; The Crew, a community-facing program built around skills, connection and participation; and DARC, which works alongside the other three to support community outcomes more broadly. You can read more about each of these programs individually elsewhere on this site, but together they represent the practical, on-the-ground side of what IRAAC does.

Behind those programs sits the governance work that doesn't always make for exciting reading but matters enormously. We operate within the Local Decision Making system, working alongside Aboriginal Affairs NSW, and alongside the regional Alliances and Assemblies that give our community a structured way to identify and progress its priorities. Being part of that system means IRAAC is expected to demonstrate — clearly, and repeatedly — that it has the governance capability to be trusted with more responsibility over time. That's not a one-off test; it's an ongoing standard, and it shapes a lot of the internal work we're currently investing in, from Board processes to reporting systems.

We want to be upfront about something: a lot of what you'll find across this website right now is a starting point rather than a finished picture. Our founding story, the full detail of the community and Country we represent, the people who lead us, and a complete account of our Board are all things we're actively building out and plan to expand as they're confirmed and ready for public release. We'd rather be transparent about that process than present something polished but incomplete in spirit. Governance, for us, isn't just about the systems we report through — it's also about being honest with our own community about where we're up to.

What we can say clearly is this: IRAAC exists because our community identified a need for stronger, more accountable Aboriginal-led organisation in this region — one capable not just of delivering programs, but of eventually helping other Aboriginal Community Organisations do the same. That ambition is bigger than any single program or report. It's the reason governance keeps showing up as a theme across everything we do, because strong governance is what turns good intentions into an organisation the community, government and funders can all genuinely rely on. We're not there yet on every front, but we're building toward it deliberately, and we'll keep sharing that journey as it unfolds.
