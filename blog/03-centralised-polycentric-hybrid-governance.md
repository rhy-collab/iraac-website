# Centralised, Polycentric or Hybrid: The Big Governance Question Facing Aboriginal Affairs in NSW

There's a conversation happening right now at the highest levels of Aboriginal Affairs policy in New South Wales, and while it can sound abstract or bureaucratic on the surface, it's actually a conversation about something very concrete: who gets to make decisions about Aboriginal communities, and how much control should sit centrally versus locally. We think it's worth explaining in plain language, because the outcome of this conversation will shape how organisations like IRAAC operate for years to come.

Governments generally organise how they deliver services and manage funding using one of a few broad models. A centralised model concentrates authority, funding decisions, reporting and performance management within a central organisation — think of a traditional public service department making most of the calls, with regional offices largely implementing decisions made elsewhere. The strengths of this approach are real: strong financial control, consistent reporting across the board, lower administrative complexity, and easier coordination across government as a whole. The trade-off is also real — reduced local autonomy, less flexibility to respond to what a specific community actually needs, and a risk that decisions end up disconnected from the realities on the ground.

At the other end of the spectrum sits what's called a polycentric model, where authority is distributed across multiple interconnected centres of decision-making, all operating within a shared framework rather than a single chain of command. International examples include the First Nations Health Authority in British Columbia, Tribal Self-Governance arrangements in the United States, and even structures like Swiss federalism. The advantages here flip the centralised model's trade-offs around: stronger local ownership, better responsiveness to community priorities, greater legitimacy, and closer alignment with self-determination principles. But it comes at a cost too — more administrative complexity, higher governance costs, more demanding reporting requirements, and the reality that capability can vary significantly from region to region.

Increasingly, governments around the world — and the current thinking in NSW — are gravitating toward a third option: hybrid governance, which tries to hold onto strong central accountability while progressively strengthening regional planning, community participation and local decision-making. It's not a compromise for its own sake; it's a recognition that centralisation and localisation aren't actually opposites you have to choose permanently between. A well-designed hybrid model can preserve the financial control and Ministerial assurance that government needs, while genuinely increasing the influence communities have over the decisions that affect them.

Here's the part we think is most important, and it's a finding that comes through clearly in recent governance analysis of this exact question: no matter which of these three pathways government ultimately chooses, the single biggest factor that improves outcomes across the whole system is the governance capability of Aboriginal Community Organisations themselves. Stronger organisations produce better reporting, stronger financial management, greater readiness for Local Decision Making, deeper community participation, and more sustainable outcomes over the long term — and that's true whether the broader system ends up more centralised, more polycentric, or something in between. In other words, the choice between these models matters, but investing in organisations like IRAAC matters regardless of which choice is made.

This is precisely why governance capability isn't just an internal administrative concern for IRAAC — it's a genuinely strategic one. Whatever direction Aboriginal Affairs NSW ultimately takes, an organisation that can demonstrate strong Board processes, clear reporting, sound financial management and real Local Decision Making readiness will be better positioned to serve its community well. We don't get to control which governance pathway government chooses. But we can control how ready IRAAC is to make the most of whichever pathway it turns out to be, and that's exactly the work we've been quietly investing in.
