# Introducing MCC: How IRAAC Is Helping Other Aboriginal Community Organisations

Of the four programs IRAAC runs, MCC — Mob and Country Connections — might be the hardest to explain in a single sentence, because it isn't a program delivered to community members in the way YouthScape or The Crew are. Instead, it's a program IRAAC delivers to other organisations, and it reflects something we believe strongly: that the best way to strengthen our sector isn't only to build IRAAC up in isolation, but to actively help other Aboriginal Community Organisations build their own capability too.

At its core, MCC is IRAAC's program for supporting other Aboriginal Community Organisations to build the governance, administration and reporting capability they need — by sharing the systems, templates and training IRAAC has already developed for itself, rather than asking every organisation to reinvent that work from scratch. If IRAAC has built a solid quarterly reporting template, a workable Board evaluation process, or clear training on what regulators like ORIC and ACNC expect, MCC is the vehicle through which that work can be handed on to another organisation that needs it, instead of sitting locked away inside IRAAC alone.

What makes MCC different from a typical top-down service delivery model is the way it's structured: support through MCC is only provided at the invitation of the organisation receiving it. This is a deliberate, relationship-based approach rather than IRAAC turning up uninvited to tell another organisation how to run itself. It reflects a simple but important principle — that governance support lands better, and respects self-determination more fully, when it's requested rather than imposed. Recent governance analysis of the sector has actually described MCC as a potential pilot worth watching closely, to test whether this kind of Aboriginal-led, peer-to-peer capability building genuinely improves outcomes across the wider sector, rather than simply channelling funding between organisations without addressing the underlying governance gaps.

Why does this matter beyond IRAAC's own walls? Because the evidence on what actually improves outcomes for Aboriginal communities keeps pointing back to the same place: organisational governance capability. Weak governance drives delayed reporting, missed acquittals, and funding relationships put at risk — problems that ripple out to affect the people programs are meant to serve. If IRAAC can help even a handful of other organisations avoid those same struggles, using systems we've already had to build and refine for ourselves anyway, the return on that effort multiplies well beyond our own community.

There's also a bigger-picture role MCC could play as the broader governance conversation about Aboriginal Affairs NSW continues to evolve. Wherever that conversation lands — whether the system becomes more centralised, more distributed, or some hybrid of the two — intermediary and capability-building structures that help organisations learn from each other are likely to remain valuable. MCC is IRAAC's practical answer to that need: not waiting for a perfect statewide structure to be designed and handed down, but building peer-to-peer capability support now, organisation to organisation, at the pace and on the terms that each organisation actually wants.

MCC is still developing, and like much of what you'll find on this site, we expect to have more concrete achievements, partner organisations and outcomes to share as the program matures. But the intent behind it is already clear and, we think, worth stating plainly: IRAAC doesn't see strengthening our own community as separate from strengthening the wider Aboriginal Community Organisation sector. They're the same project, approached from two directions at once.
