# Why Governance Capability Is the Single Most Important Investment in Our Sector

If you asked most people what determines whether an Aboriginal Community Organisation succeeds, they'd probably point to funding, or programs, or staff numbers. Those things all matter, but recent governance analysis of the sector points to something less visible and, frankly, less exciting to talk about: governance capability. We want to explain why this unglamorous-sounding thing might actually be the most important lever available for improving outcomes across our entire sector — and why IRAAC has made it such a central part of what we do.

Governance capability, in plain terms, is an organisation's ability to govern itself well: to make sound decisions, manage resources responsibly, report accurately and on time, and participate effectively in the systems it operates within, like Local Decision Making. It sounds procedural, but the evidence on what happens when it's missing is stark. National auditing has found that reporting-related issues alone caused the overwhelming majority of funding agreement breaches in reviews of capacity development for Indigenous service delivery — we're talking numbers in the high nineties, as a percentage of breaches. Separately, research into the sector has identified thousands of funding agreements spread across hundreds of Indigenous organisations, each one a point where weak governance can translate into a missed report, a delayed acquittal, or a funding relationship put at risk.

The costs of weak governance are not abstract. They show up as delayed acquittals — the process of proving that funding has actually been spent for its approved purpose — as duplicated administration, as compliance interventions from funders and regulators, as programs stalling because paperwork wasn't right, and ultimately as reduced readiness to take on the greater responsibility that Local Decision Making is meant to offer. Every one of those outcomes affects real people: young people whose program gets delayed, community members whose priorities don't get progressed, staff who spend their time firefighting compliance problems instead of doing the work they actually joined the organisation to do.

The flip side is just as real. Strong governance reduces cost and risk precisely by improving the things that weak governance undermines — reporting quality, financial management, organisational stability, community participation, and value for money for the funders who are ultimately trusting organisations like ours with public money. And here's the finding we think matters most: this holds true regardless of which broader governance model government eventually settles on for the sector. Whether the system becomes more centralised, more polycentric, or something hybrid in between, stronger Aboriginal Community Organisations improve outcomes under every single one of those scenarios. Governance capability isn't a bet on one particular policy direction — it's a safe investment no matter which direction things go.

This is exactly the thinking behind IRAAC's MCC program — Mob and Country Connections — which exists specifically to help other Aboriginal Community Organisations build this same capability, using the systems, templates and training IRAAC has developed for itself, at the invitation of the organisation receiving support. It's also why so much of our own internal work over the past period has gone into things that might seem unglamorous from the outside: standing Board reporting frameworks, annual independent Board evaluations, and a clear external reporting calendar covering quarterly reports, acquittals and audits. None of that is exciting to write about. All of it is, according to the evidence, exactly where the biggest gains for our community actually come from.

We'd encourage anyone thinking about the future of the sector — funders, government, other Aboriginal Community Organisations, and our own community members — to hold onto this idea. The conversation about which governance model is best for Aboriginal Affairs NSW will keep happening, and it's an important conversation. But the surer bet, the one that pays off no matter how that bigger conversation resolves, is building governance capability at the organisational level, one organisation at a time. That's the work IRAAC has chosen to lead by example on, and it's the work we hope to help other organisations take on too.
