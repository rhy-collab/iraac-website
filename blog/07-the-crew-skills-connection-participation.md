# The Crew: Building Skills, Connection and Participation in Our Community

Not every program needs a complicated explanation, and The Crew is a good example of that. It's IRAAC's community-facing program built around three simple but powerful things: skills, connection and participation. In a sector where so much energy goes into governance frameworks, funding arrangements and reporting obligations, it's worth pausing on a program whose entire purpose is much more direct — bringing people together and helping them build capability they can carry with them.

The Crew sits within IRAAC's broader suite of four programs, alongside MCC, YouthScape and DARC, and it's specifically oriented toward the community more broadly rather than any single age group or focus area. The idea is straightforward: create a program where people can build practical skills, strengthen their connection to community, and participate in something that has real value, both to themselves individually and to the community collectively. Participation, in particular, matters more than it might first appear — one of the clearest findings from recent governance work in this sector is that genuine community participation is one of the strongest indicators of whether a governance system, and the organisation running it, is actually working the way it's meant to.

As with a couple of our other programs, we want to be upfront that the specific activities, the people The Crew supports, and the detailed outcomes are still being built out and documented for this website. That's a deliberate choice rather than an oversight — we'd rather describe The Crew honestly, as a program still developing its full public story, than dress it up with detail that isn't genuine yet. What we can say with confidence is the intent behind it: a program that exists purely to build people up, through skills and connection, without requiring a young age bracket or a specific eligibility category to take part.

Funding for The Crew, like YouthScape and MCC, comes through Local Decision Making and Aboriginal Affairs NSW funding arrangements, with governance and capability work built directly into the program's budget. That structure matters more than it might seem. It means The Crew isn't just a nice idea sitting alongside IRAAC's compliance and reporting work — it's directly tied into the same governance systems that let IRAAC demonstrate to funders, regulators like ORIC and ACNC, and community itself that the program is being run properly and delivering genuine value. Good governance, in other words, is what protects programs like The Crew and keeps them funded and running.

There's also a quieter but important role The Crew plays in IRAAC's bigger picture. Programs built around participation and connection generate exactly the kind of community engagement that strengthens Local Decision Making — the more people who are genuinely connected to and participating in community life through programs like this, the stronger the voice IRAAC can bring to Alliances, Assemblies and the broader system that's meant to reflect community priorities back into government decision-making.

As with our other still-developing programs, we're committed to sharing more real detail about The Crew as it becomes available — who it supports, what activities it runs, and what outcomes it's achieving. For now, know this: it exists because building skills and strengthening participation, person by person, is one of the most direct ways an organisation like IRAAC can serve its community.
