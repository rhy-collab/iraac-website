# What Is Local Decision Making, and Why Does It Matter to Our Community?

If you've spent any time on this website, you'll have noticed that almost everything IRAAC does gets described as sitting "within the Local Decision Making framework." It's a phrase that gets used a lot, in government documents and in our own reporting, and it's easy for it to become background noise — a bit of policy language that washes over you without really landing. We wanted to slow down and explain, in plain terms, what Local Decision Making actually is, why it exists, and why it matters so much to how IRAAC operates day to day.

At its heart, Local Decision Making, or LDM, is a New South Wales Government framework built on a fairly simple idea: Aboriginal communities should have a genuine say in the decisions that affect them, rather than having those decisions made entirely by people and departments far removed from community life. For a long time, the story of Aboriginal affairs administration in this country has been one of centralised control — decisions made in Sydney offices, policies designed without local input, and programs rolled out the same way in every community regardless of what that community actually needed. LDM was designed as a deliberate shift away from that pattern, recognising that the people best placed to identify a community's priorities are the people who live in that community.

In practice, this happens through structures called Alliances and Assemblies. Alliances are regional Aboriginal governance structures that support engagement, priority identification and planning across a broader area, while Assemblies are more local community forums or representative structures that give people a direct way to participate and have their voice carry legitimacy. Together, these structures form what's sometimes called the "participatory architecture" of the system — the practical mechanism through which community priorities get identified, discussed and fed upward into decisions that Aboriginal Affairs NSW and government ultimately act on. IRAAC works alongside these Alliances and Assemblies as part of how we progress the priorities our own community identifies.

Why does this matter for an organisation like IRAAC specifically? Because everything we do — our programs, our governance systems, the way we report to funders — exists inside this framework, and being part of it isn't passive. LDM isn't just a policy that happens to us; it's something IRAAC is actively expected to demonstrate readiness for. That means showing that our governance is strong enough, our reporting is clear enough, and our organisation is stable enough to be trusted with more responsibility and more local decision-making power over time. It's less like a badge we're given and more like a standard we have to keep earning.

There's an honest tension built into this system, and it's worth naming rather than glossing over. Governments understandably want strong accountability for public money — they need to know funds are spent properly, outcomes are achieved, and there's someone to answer to if things go wrong. Communities, just as understandably, want meaningful control over decisions that shape their own lives, not just a consultation exercise before someone else decides anyway. Local Decision Making is essentially the NSW Government's attempt to hold both of those things at once: strong enough oversight to satisfy Ministers and Treasury, while genuinely shifting real influence out to Alliances, Assemblies and organisations like IRAAC. Whether that balance is being struck well, and how it should evolve, is an active and ongoing conversation — one that recent governance reviews have been specifically exploring.

For our community, the practical upshot is this: the stronger IRAAC's own governance and reporting becomes, the more Local Decision Making can actually deliver on its promise. A framework that's designed to hand more say to community only works if the organisations representing that community can show, clearly and consistently, that they're ready to use it well. That's precisely why you'll see us talking so much about governance, Board processes and reporting standards elsewhere on this site — it isn't administrative box-ticking for its own sake. It's the groundwork that makes Local Decision Making mean something real, both for IRAAC and for the community we exist to serve.
