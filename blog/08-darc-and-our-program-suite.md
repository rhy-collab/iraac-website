# DARC and IRAAC's Program Suite: What Ties Our Work Together

We've written separately about MCC, YouthScape and The Crew, and it would be easy to treat DARC as simply the fourth item on a list. But we think it's worth pausing on DARC specifically, and on the broader question it raises: what actually ties IRAAC's four programs together into something more than a collection of separate initiatives?

DARC is part of IRAAC's program suite, working alongside MCC, YouthScape and The Crew to support community outcomes. We'll be honest that, of all our programs, DARC is currently the least publicly documented — its specific focus, activities and outcomes are still being defined and confirmed before we share more detail here. Rather than fill that gap with vague language that sounds more finished than it is, we'd rather name it plainly: DARC exists, it's an active part of how IRAAC supports community, and more detail is coming as it's ready to be shared responsibly.

What we can talk about with more confidence is the logic that connects DARC to IRAAC's other three programs, because it's the same logic that runs through everything we do. Each program addresses a different slice of what community wellbeing requires — MCC strengthens other organisations' governance capability, YouthScape connects young people to culture and opportunity, The Crew builds skills and participation across the wider community — and DARC rounds out that suite by supporting community outcomes in its own particular way. None of these programs exist in isolation from each other or from IRAAC's governance work. They're funded through the same Local Decision Making and Aboriginal Affairs NSW arrangements, they're each expected to demonstrate the same standard of accountability, and they all draw on the same underlying organisational capability that IRAAC has been deliberately investing in.

This is actually a good moment to explain something about how IRAAC thinks about its programs as a group, rather than one at a time. Governance analysis of our sector consistently points to a simple but important insight: an Aboriginal Community Organisation with strong governance capability improves outcomes across every program it runs, not just one. A Board that meets regularly with clear agendas and tracked decisions, a reporting calendar that keeps every program's acquittals and audits on schedule, training that makes sure staff understand what ORIC, ACNC and Aboriginal Affairs NSW actually expect — none of that is specific to MCC, or YouthScape, or The Crew, or DARC. It's shared infrastructure that lifts all four at once. In that sense, DARC benefits from every governance improvement IRAAC makes for any of its programs, and vice versa.

There's a practical reason this matters for funders and community alike. When a program is under-resourced on the governance side, it tends to show up as delayed reporting, missed acquittals, or funding relationships put under strain — problems that can stall a program regardless of how good its underlying work actually is. By building governance capability at the organisational level rather than program by program, IRAAC is trying to make sure DARC has the same solid foundation underneath it as our more established programs, even while its public profile is still catching up.

We'll keep expanding on DARC specifically as its focus and activities are confirmed for public release. In the meantime, if there's one thing worth taking from this post, it's that IRAAC's four programs were never meant to be read as four unrelated initiatives. They're four expressions of the same underlying commitment — to community, to capability, and to the kind of governance that makes sure good intentions actually translate into outcomes people can rely on.
