# How IRAAC Reports, and Why That Builds Trust

Reporting is not the part of IRAAC's work that inspires anyone — nobody joins an Aboriginal Community Organisation because they're passionate about quarterly reports and audit schedules. But we've written enough about governance capability on this site now to owe our community a straightforward explanation of what IRAAC actually reports, to whom, and why we think this unglamorous discipline is one of the most important things we do.

IRAAC reports to a genuinely wide range of people and bodies, because that's simply what being a well-run Aboriginal Community Organisation requires. We report to Aboriginal Affairs NSW as our primary state government partner and funder. We report to ORIC, the regulator responsible for corporate compliance for Indigenous corporations, and separately to the ACNC, which covers our obligations as a registered charity. We report within the Local Decision Making framework itself, demonstrating our readiness to take on the responsibility that framework is designed to hand to community. And we report to auditors, other government agencies, and the various funders whose support keeps our four programs running. It's a genuinely demanding list, and each relationship comes with its own expectations and its own calendar.

In practical terms, this means IRAAC works to a reporting rhythm covering quarterly progress reports, annual reports, program acquittals — the process of proving that funding was spent for its approved purpose — audit reports, ORIC reports, ACNC reports and reports to individual funding bodies. Behind all of that sits a Board that meets regularly using a standard agenda, minutes, decisions and actions framework, which sounds procedural but actually matters enormously in practice: it's what makes it possible for the Board to track its own decisions properly, and what makes it possible for IRAAC to demonstrate to funders and regulators that governance is being taken seriously, rather than simply asserted. On top of that, IRAAC runs an annual independent Board evaluation to check and improve our own governance every year, and Board members, staff and community members all receive governance training relevant to their specific role.

We think it's worth explaining why this matters beyond simple compliance, because the evidence on this point is genuinely striking. National auditing work has found that reporting-related issues alone caused the vast majority of funding agreement breaches identified in reviews of Indigenous service delivery — figures in the high nineties as a percentage of all breaches found. Put simply, most of the trouble organisations like ours can get into with funders isn't caused by bad intentions or poor program delivery. It's caused by reporting failures: a missed deadline, an unclear acquittal, a Board decision that wasn't properly documented. That's actually good news in a strange way, because it means the biggest risk to an organisation's stability is also one of the most fixable, if you invest properly in the systems and discipline required.

There's a trust dimension here too, and it runs in more than one direction. Strong, consistent reporting is what gives Aboriginal Affairs NSW and other funders the confidence to keep investing in IRAAC's programs, and eventually to hand over more genuine decision-making authority under Local Decision Making. But it's also what gives our own community confidence that IRAAC is being run properly with the resources entrusted to us — that funding intended for young people, for community skills and connection, for supporting other organisations through MCC, is actually reaching those purposes and being accounted for honestly.

We don't expect anyone outside IRAAC to get excited about our reporting calendar. But we do want our community to know it exists, to understand roughly what it involves, and to see why we treat it as seriously as we do. Good governance and good reporting aren't the opposite of good community work — they're what protects it, funds it, and eventually earns it the trust to grow.
