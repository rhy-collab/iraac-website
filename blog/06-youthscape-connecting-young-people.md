# YouthScape: Connecting Our Young People With Culture, Opportunity and Support

Of everything IRAAC does, YouthScape is probably the program with the most obvious long-term stakes. Governance frameworks and reporting systems matter enormously for an organisation's sustainability, but a community's future is ultimately carried by its young people — and YouthScape exists to make sure they're genuinely connected to culture, opportunity and support as they grow.

YouthScape is IRAAC's program specifically focused on young people in the community. At its simplest, the idea is to build real connection: linking young people back to culture and Country, opening doors to opportunities they might not otherwise access, and providing the kind of support that helps them navigate the challenges young people in any community face, with the added weight that comes from growing up as part of a community still working to secure stronger self-determination and recognition. It sits within IRAAC's broader program suite alongside MCC, The Crew and DARC, each addressing a different part of what community wellbeing actually requires.

We want to be honest that YouthScape, like several of our programs, is still in a relatively early stage of being fully documented and publicly described. The specific activities, the age groups involved, and the outcomes we're working toward are all things we're actively building out, and we'd rather share that development openly than present a polished-sounding program that overstates where we currently are. What we can say is that the underlying commitment is real and central to why IRAAC exists at all: an Aboriginal Community Organisation that isn't actively investing in its young people isn't really investing in its own future.

There's also a governance thread running underneath YouthScape that's worth drawing out, even in a program built around young people rather than paperwork. Programs like YouthScape are funded through Local Decision Making and Aboriginal Affairs NSW funding arrangements, and IRAAC has structured things so that governance and capability work is built into each program's budget rather than treated as a separate concern. That means the same discipline that goes into IRAAC's Board reporting and financial management also underpins YouthScape — because a program that can't demonstrate it's well run and properly accounted for is a program at risk of losing the funding that keeps it running for young people who need it.

YouthScape is also part of why IRAAC takes the MCC program so seriously. As we build out YouthScape's systems, templates and lessons learned, that work doesn't have to stay locked inside IRAAC. If another Aboriginal Community Organisation wants to run something similar for their own young people, the governance and program-delivery groundwork IRAAC has already done for YouthScape becomes something we can share, rather than something every organisation has to build in isolation.

We know families and community members will want to see more — specific activities, real stories, actual outcomes — and that's exactly where this program is headed. As YouthScape's work develops and we're able to share genuine results rather than intentions, we'll keep updating this space. For now, the commitment stands clearly: our young people deserve connection to culture, real opportunity, and support that meets them where they are, and YouthScape is IRAAC's answer to that responsibility.
