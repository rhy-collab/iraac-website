# What Role Could NCARA Play? Coordination Without Losing Local Voice

Not every idea shaping the future of Aboriginal Affairs in New South Wales is already settled and operating. Some are still being actively discussed, tested and refined — and one of the more interesting of these is NCARA, a proposed statewide Aboriginal coordinating structure that keeps coming up in current governance thinking about the sector. We wanted to explain what NCARA is being discussed as, what it isn't, and why the way it's ultimately defined matters to organisations like IRAAC.

NCARA doesn't currently exist as a fixed, fully operational body with settled powers — it's best understood as a structure whose role is still being worked out, sitting conceptually between Aboriginal Affairs NSW at the state government level and the regional Alliances and Assemblies that already give community a structured voice locally. The thinking behind it draws on international experience, which suggests that intermediary structures — bodies that sit between government and community organisations without being either one — can genuinely help connect the two, provided they're designed carefully.

The possible value of NCARA, as it's currently being discussed, centres on a few specific functions: acting as a representative voice that provides a statewide Aboriginal perspective, serving as a coordination body that helps different organisations and regions work together rather than in isolation, and functioning as a shared learning network where lessons and successful approaches from one part of the sector can reach others more easily. Under a hybrid governance model — the kind that tries to balance strong central accountability with genuine local participation — Aboriginal Affairs NSW would likely retain funding assurance, audit and Ministerial reporting responsibilities, while NCARA would focus on the coordination, capability-building and shared-learning side of things.

Here's the part that current thinking is genuinely careful about, and we think it's the most important thing for community to understand: the risk with any new coordinating structure is that it duplicates, rather than complements, the roles Alliances and Assemblies already play. As governance becomes more distributed across a system, responsibilities need to become clearer, not murkier — who decides what, who reports outcomes, who's accountable, and who resolves disputes all need sharper definition, not additional layers of ambiguity. Without that clarity, adding a new structure like NCARA into the mix risks creating exactly the kind of fragmentation, duplication and unintended competition that Local Decision Making is meant to reduce, not increase.

This is why NCARA's potential value is framed specifically around enabling — coordination, shared learning, system cohesion — rather than around exercising direct control over funding, priorities or decisions that Alliances and Assemblies already handle at the regional and community level. Get that framing right, and NCARA could genuinely help address a real gap: right now, there isn't always an easy way for the lessons IRAAC learns from running MCC, for example, to reach other organisations doing similar work elsewhere in the state, or for successful governance approaches to spread across the sector rather than being reinvented independently by every organisation that needs them.

IRAAC doesn't have a formal role in deciding whether or how NCARA is established — that's a decision for Aboriginal Affairs NSW and government more broadly, informed by consultation across the sector. But we're watching this conversation closely, because a well-designed coordinating structure could meaningfully support the exact kind of peer-to-peer capability building that programs like our own MCC are already trying to do. We'll keep sharing updates on this as the thinking develops, because how NCARA is ultimately shaped is one of the more consequential open questions for the future of Aboriginal governance in this state.
